In the early hours of September 19, 1999, a drunken driver returning from a party fell asleep as his SUV swerved into a lane of oncoming traffic, hitting head-on the car Jacqueline Saburido, 20, and three others were in, killing two and trapping Saburido in the fiery wreak that left her disfigured beyond recognition. Saburido, from Venezuela, was studying English in Austin. After countless surgeries and national and international media coverage and public service campaigns, a dispirited Saburido lives in an apartment with her father and caregiver in Caracas. Reginald Stephey, 18, the drunken driver, was found guilty of two counts of intoxication manslaughter and was sentenced to seven years in prison. He served all seven years.

Austin is a drinking town with a football problem
According to the National Highway Traffic Safety Administration, Texas led the nation in 2009 in drunken-driving fatalities, and ranks among the 10 states that have done the least to prevent these fatalities. More alcohol is sold per capita in Austin than in any city in Texas, and sales have more than doubled, from $314 million in 2004 to $689 million in 2013, excluding sales at liquor and grocery stores. As Austin grows, so does its alcohol sales. State data show that sales of beer, wine, and mixed drinks were $600 million more than last year, and since 2014 alcohol sales in Austin have increased almost 10 percent, while sales statewide grew only six percent.

Drunken driving among college students is a national epidemic
Nationally, 2.8 million college students have driven while intoxicated, says the National Institute on Alcohol Abuse and Alcoholism, who in 2001 estimated 2.1 million students drove drunk. This has not escaped the attention of college and university presidents and chancellors, some of who support the Amethyst Initiative. (Amethyst is from the Greek amethystos, which means remedy against drunkenness.) This Initiative encourages university presidents and their peers to lobby elected officials to encourage public debate over the 21-year-old drinking age, which they believe has had the unintended consequence of fostering irresponsible drinking among college students and putting them at risk for a host of other threats. As the Austin-American Statesman put it, the reckless use of alcohol on college campuses nationally is resulting in an estimated 1,700 deaths yearly, 97,000 incidents of sexual assault or date rape, 599,000 injuries, and 696,000 assaults.
Read that again.

Is it any wonder that the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention has declared alcohol abuse on college campuses a national epidemic? Is it any wonder that college students spend more money on alcoholic beverages than textbooks? To put this into perspective, if these cans were stacked end-to-end upon each other, they would reach the moon and extend 70,000 miles beyond. University presidents have the authority to mandate that comprehensive and innovative alcohol-abuse education programs are planned strategically and implemented successfully across their campuses, and serve as catalysts to promote ownership of these programs among students, faculty, and staff. By virtue of their positions, they have the visibility to reach other stakeholders, such as students, parents, communities, legislators, and alumni, to mention a few. But they our help to break the silence that creates the culture of alcohol abuse on and near our campus. It all starts with us.
